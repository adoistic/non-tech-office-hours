# Office Hours — for non-technical founders with a technical co-founder

This is a portable office-hours skill calibrated to a specific situation: **you are a non-technical founder, you have a technical co-founder, and your co-founder is working in the AI-native paradigm of 2026** (software factories, token-maxing, closed-loop ops, the operating model described in `02_AI_NATIVE_CONTEXT.md`). You're not writing code. Your job is to be unmistakably clear about the customer, the problem, the wedge, the demand, the story, the pricing, the distribution, and the operating-model decisions only you can make. Your co-founder will figure out the *how* — what to build, how to build it, and what stack to use.

This is the conversation a sharp partner, advisor, or first investor would have with you before letting your co-founder spend even three weeks building.

## How to use it

1. Open a Claude.ai conversation. Talk about what you're building. One message or twenty — whatever context you've already got.
2. When you're ready for office hours, attach **all the markdown files in this folder** to your next message.
3. Send something like:
   > "Run office hours on what we've been discussing. Start by reading `00_START_HERE.md`."

The partner will:
- Read the operating context (`02_AI_NATIVE_CONTEXT.md`) so it's calibrated to AI-native production economics, not 2023 assumptions.
- Read your prior conversation.
- Reflect back what it understands, ask the conviction check once, and continue from where you left off.
- Run six forcing questions one at a time. Push hard on each.
- Force premise challenge (5–7 premises, including AI-native premises where relevant).
- Force three real alternatives — one no-code, one minimum AI-native build, one creative / AI-native rebuild.
- Lock a strategy using Roger Martin's Playing-to-Win framework, with a five-bucket "what must be true" backup.
- Draft **two output documents** in plain English with ASCII flowcharts in the second.
- Run five autonomous review passes over both documents (clarity, justification, zero-hallucination, completeness, cross-document consistency).
- Read back the documents with you and confirm you'll email the co-founder doc this week.

## The output

**Two documents, copied out of the chat:**

- **`FOR_YOU.md`** — your owned ground. Problem, customer, demand, wedge, premises, strategy (winning aspiration → where to play → how to win → capabilities → management systems → what-must-be-true watchlist), distribution, pricing, success criteria 30/90, your assignment for this week, and a few observational bullets about how you think.

- **`FOR_YOUR_CO_FOUNDER.md`** — your co-founder's input document. Every workflow described in absolute detail (trigger → steps in plain English → ASCII flowchart → edge cases → success criteria), plus your operating-model decisions (closed-loop ops position, token-spend position, team-archetype shape for the first 5 hires, dashboards required from week one), plus explicitly-out-of-scope items, plus the technical questions you parked during the session for the co-founder to answer using their own brainstorming.

**Important — what's NOT in either document:** technology, frameworks, languages, databases, hosting choices, architecture style. Those are the co-founder's decisions to make, using their own brainstorming or office-hours skill, once they've internalized this document. The non-technical founder names workflows and operating-model choices; the technical co-founder picks the stack.

## The discipline

This is stricter than a normal brainstorming chat. Expect:

- **No vague answers accepted.** If you say "small businesses," you'll get pushed until you name a specific human at a specific company.
- **Behavior beats interest.** Waitlists, "people love the idea," "VCs are excited" — none counts. The only evidence is money or behavior.
- **One question at a time.** No batching. The hard questions are the value.
- **Plain language only.** Jargon gets unpacked. If a term sneaks in, it gets defined or removed.
- **Clarity is the bottleneck.** In an AI-native world, your co-founder can ship the wrong thing very fast. The premium on your clarity has gone up, not down. Fuzziness is the most expensive defect in the room.
- **Strategy is held to a different standard than the rest.** Phase 4 (Strategy) is the strictest on coherence and most forgiving on certainty. You can't prove a strategy in advance — you can only articulate it clearly, back it up with what-must-be-true logic, and own the bet.
- **The partner self-reviews before showing you the output.** Five explicit passes — clarity, justification, zero-hallucination, completeness, cross-document consistency. Anything plausible-sounding but not actually traceable to something you said gets removed.
- **The assignment is mandatory.** Every session ends with one concrete thing you should do this week — usually a real customer conversation.

## The technical co-founder handoff

You have a technical co-founder. That means a clean division of labor:

| You own | They own |
| --- | --- |
| Who the customer is | What stack, framework, database |
| What problem they have | Architecture choices |
| Why now | What's hard vs. easy to build |
| What the wedge is | Deployment and operations |
| How you'll reach them | Scaling and reliability |
| Pricing and packaging | The build itself |
| **The operating model: closed-loop, token-spend, archetypes** | The infrastructure that delivers it |

If a technical question comes up in this session, the partner will **note it and park it** for you to take back to your co-founder — *but not too eagerly.* The point is to keep you focused on the business side.

What you *can't* do is hide behind the technical co-founder. If you try to skip past customer specificity or demand evidence by saying "my co-founder will figure that out" — the partner pushes back. Those are *your* questions to answer.

## What you'll walk out with

- **Two documents in plain English** that you can copy out of the chat: one for you, one for your co-founder.
- **One concrete assignment** for this week — usually a customer conversation, an observation, a manual test, or (if you haven't already) two hours sitting with the coding agents to develop personal conviction.
- **A short list of resources** tailored to what came up in the conversation.
- A **non-negotiable handoff:** email `FOR_YOUR_CO_FOUNDER.md` to your co-founder this week. The next conversation you have with them should be about it.

## Why this exists

In 2026, the bottleneck of an early-stage startup is no longer "can we build it?" It is "do we know what to build?" A 1,000x engineer with a fuzzy spec produces 1,000x more wrong product. Same engineer, different outcome. The variable is the non-technical founder's clarity. This skill exists to make that clarity inevitable — by being stricter than the founder would be alone, and clearer than the partner would be without it.
