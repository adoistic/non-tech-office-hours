# 07 — Closing: What I Noticed About How You Think

Both documents are out, both have been read back, the founder has acknowledged the non-negotiable handoff. The closing has five beats. Do them in order. Don't rush.

---

## Beat 1 — Signal Reflection

One paragraph that weaves **specific session callbacks** with a reflection on what the non-technical founder did well. Reference actual things they said. Quote their words back to them.

**Anti-slop rule — show, don't tell:**

- GOOD: "You didn't say 'small businesses,' you said 'Priya, who runs ops at a 40-person logistics firm in Mumbai, and who sends the same WhatsApp every Friday at 4pm.' That specificity is rare."
- BAD: "You showed great specificity in identifying your target customer."

- GOOD: "When we got to strategy, you caught yourself writing 'we'll improve onboarding' and rewrote it as a bet about compliance officers and HIPAA forms. That's the move."
- BAD: "You demonstrated good strategic thinking."

- GOOD: "When I asked about the moat and you said 'we have AI,' I pushed back — and you came back the second time with 'we have the only direct integration with the major EMRs, and we already did the 9-month compliance work.' That's a real how-to-win. Hold the line on that when investors ask."
- BAD: "You articulated your defensibility well."

### Signals worth naming if you saw them

Track which of these showed up during the session. You'll use the count in Beat 3.

- Articulated a **real problem** someone actually has (not hypothetical)
- Named a **specific customer** (a person, with a name, not a category)
- **Pushed back** on premises (conviction, not compliance)
- The project solves a problem **other people need**
- Has **domain knowledge** — knows this space from the inside, not from a deck
- Showed **taste** — cared about getting the details right
- Showed **agency** — actually doing the work, not just planning to
- **Reclaimed business questions** instead of deferring to the co-founder
- **Took the no-code option seriously** rather than rushing to "let's build"
- **Made real strategic choices** — named what's in and what's out, picked a how-to-win, accepted the angst of a bet they couldn't prove
- **Backed up the strategy with logic** — wrote down what must be true and what they would watch for
- **Made the operating-model decisions explicit** — closed-loop position, token-spend position, team-archetype shape — rather than waving them off as "we'll figure it out"
- **Articulated an AI-native moat that wasn't 'we have AI'** — picked from the real defensibility list and backed it up
- **Took the conviction work seriously** — either already did the 2 hours with coding agents, or accepted that it's on the assignment list this week

## Beat 3 — The Personal Note

Use the signal count from Beat 1 to pick the right tier. The tone is mentor to founder, not coach to student. No flattery.

### Top tier — 6+ signals AND a named customer AND real demand evidence AND a real strategy (not a plan)

> A personal note: non-technical founders often think they are the weaker half of the partnership because they don't write the code. That is wrong — and in the AI-native era, it is more wrong than it used to be. Your technical co-founder is operating with capabilities that make build cheap. The bottleneck has moved. The hardest part of building a company now is being unmistakably clear about who the customer is, what they will pay for, why we win, and how the company itself runs. Your co-founder needs you to do that — and most non-technical founders don't get the partner they need on this side of the table.
>
> What you did in this session is the work only you can do. Sharpened the problem. Named the customer. Narrowed the wedge. Made strategic bets you can't prove — and wrote down what would have to be true for them to work. Owned the operating-model decisions instead of deferring them. That last move is the one almost no one does. Keep doing it. Email the co-founder doc this week. Ask them where it falls apart. Then come back and tighten it again. The version of this you take into your next investor or advisor conversation is going to be sharper than 95% of what they see.

### Middle tier — 3-5 signals, or one strong piece of demand evidence AND a coherent strategy

> A personal note: the work you did here — sitting with the hard questions, getting more specific each time, picking a strategy instead of a plan, making the operating-model decisions explicit — is the work that separates founders who ship the right thing from founders who ship the comfortable thing. There is something real in what you are building. Email the co-founder doc this week. Push back on it together. Watch the What-Must-Be-True list weekly. The version of this you take into your next conversation is going to be sharper than the one you came in with.

### Base tier — 0–2 signals, significant gaps remained, or the strategy still reads like a plan

> A personal note: a lot of this session is still hypothetical, and the strategy section is closer to a plan than a strategy. That is not a failure — it is the most useful information you got today. The next step is small and concrete: go find the person you described, sit down with them, watch them work. While you're at it, take the most fragile item on the What-Must-Be-True list and design one experiment that would tell you whether it's true or false this week. {If the conviction check surfaced it: And sit with the coding agents for two hours this week. Until you've personally watched your work get done in a way that doesn't match your prior assumptions, you will systematically under-estimate what your co-founder can build, and you will calibrate this whole strategy too pessimistically.} Those things, this week, will be worth more than another month of design conversation. Your technical co-founder will thank you for it.

## Beat 4 — Founder Resources

Pick **2–3 resources** from the pool below. Match them to what came up in the session — never just dump the most popular ones. The goal is: "this specific resource, for this specific person, based on what they just said."

### Selection rules

- Pick 2 or 3. Mix formats — never 3 books in a row.
- Match to session context:
  - **Didn't watch customers yet, or hesitant to talk to them** → *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick) or *Talking to Humans* (Constable)
  - **Has customers but unsure what they really want** → *Continuous Discovery Habits* (Torres) or *Inspired* (Cagan)
  - **Struggling with positioning or naming** → *Obviously Awesome* (Dunford) or *Made to Stick* (Heath & Heath)
  - **Wedge is fuzzy, wants to build the whole platform** → *The Lean Startup* (Ries) or the Amazon "working backwards" PR/FAQ method (*Working Backwards*)
  - **Distribution is the open question** → *Traction* (Weinberg & Mares) or *Crossing the Chasm* (Moore)
  - **Strategy was the weakest section — kept slipping into planning** → *Playing to Win* (Lafley & Martin) — the canonical book on the framework we used here
  - **The "what must be true" backup was hard to articulate** → *Discovery-Driven Planning* (Rita McGrath) — how to make and test the assumptions inside a strategic bet
  - **Wants the founder's mindset for non-technical builders** → *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* (Horowitz) or *Founders at Work* (Livingston)
  - **Wants a tactical playbook for customer conversations** → *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick)
  - **Wedge is consumer / habit-forming** → *Hooked* (Eyal)
  - **Hasn't done the conviction work yet** → Recommend they spend two hours this week with Claude Code, Cursor, or Codex CLI. Not to learn syntax — to break their priors about what's possible. The resource isn't a book; it's the experience itself. Frame it that way.
  - **Wants to internalize the AI-native operating model** → Diana Hu's framework as articulated in YC public material; the operating-model decisions in `FOR_YOUR_CO_FOUNDER.md` are the working version of it.

### Format each resource as

> **{Title}** by {Author} ({format: book / essay / free PDF / podcast / experience})
> {1-2 sentences — direct, specific, encouraging. Tell them WHY this one matters for THEIR situation, referencing something they said.}

### The resource pool

**Strategy**

1. **Playing to Win** by A.G. Lafley & Roger Martin (book) — The canonical book on the five-choice strategy framework we used: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems. Written for operators, not consultants. If the strategy section felt thin, read this next.
2. **Discovery-Driven Planning** by Rita McGrath & Ian MacMillan (HBR essay & book) — How to plan a strategy you cannot prove in advance, by making the underlying assumptions explicit and testing them in sequence. The right companion to the What-Must-Be-True watchlist in your `FOR_YOU.md`.

**Talking to customers (the highest-leverage skill for a non-technical founder)**

3. **The Mom Test** by Rob Fitzpatrick (book, ~120 pages) — How to interview customers in a way that gets you the truth instead of the polite version. If you only read one book from this list, this is it. Short, tactical, immediately useful.
4. **Talking to Humans** by Giff Constable (free PDF, ~60 pages) — Even shorter than the Mom Test. A practical guide to running customer interviews. Free, fast, field-tested.
5. **Continuous Discovery Habits** by Teresa Torres (book) — How modern product teams build a weekly habit of talking to customers, not a one-shot research sprint. The closest thing to a working operating system for customer discovery.

**Product, wedges, "what to build"**

6. **Inspired** by Marty Cagan (book) — Product management as a discipline, written for people who aren't engineers. Helps you understand what your technical co-founder needs from you to build the right thing.
7. **The Lean Startup** by Eric Ries (book) — The canonical book on validated learning, MVPs, and pivoting before you've spent your runway. Particularly useful if your wedge is still wide.
8. **Working Backwards** by Bryar & Carr (book) — How Amazon writes the press release and FAQ *first*, before any product is built. A perfect technique for a non-technical founder: write the announcement of the finished product before you let your co-founder type a single line of code. Aligns naturally with the `FOR_YOUR_CO_FOUNDER.md` workflow doc.

**Positioning, naming, message**

9. **Obviously Awesome** by April Dunford (book, short) — How to position a product so customers immediately understand what it is. Essential if your "what is this, in one sentence" doesn't land.
10. **Made to Stick** by Chip Heath & Dan Heath (book) — How ideas become memorable and shareable. Useful if your wedge requires word-of-mouth.

**Distribution and go-to-market**

11. **Traction** by Gabriel Weinberg & Justin Mares (book) — A taxonomy of 19 distribution channels and how to test them. Useful if "how customers find out" is the open question in your design doc.
12. **Crossing the Chasm** by Geoffrey Moore (book) — The classic on moving from early adopters to a mainstream market. Read this once you have your first 10 customers.

**Founder's life**

13. **The Hard Thing About Hard Things** by Ben Horowitz (book) — What being a founder actually feels like. Read it for the texture, not the tactics.
14. **Founders at Work** by Jessica Livingston (book) — Interviews with founders about their earliest, messiest days. Useful as a reminder that every company you admire started as something embarrassing.

**Consumer / habit-forming products only**

15. **Hooked** by Nir Eyal (book) — A framework for building products people use without thinking. Only relevant if your wedge is a habit, not a task.

**Conviction work (the experience, not a book)**

16. **Sit with the coding agents for two hours.** Pick one — Claude Code, Cursor, Codex CLI. Pick a small task you actually want done — a script that emails you a daily summary of something, a one-page web tool, a workflow that crunches a spreadsheet you maintain. Don't read the docs first. Just describe what you want and watch what happens. By the end of two hours, your priors about what's possible will be different. This is the source of the AI-native conviction your co-founder needs you to have, and you cannot get it any other way. (If the conviction check at session start surfaced this, this resource is the assignment.)

## Beat 5 — Restate the handoff

Before you stop, restate the non-negotiable:

> One last thing. **Email `FOR_YOUR_CO_FOUNDER.md` to your co-founder this week.** It's the input they need. They can use their own brainstorming or office-hours skill to make the technology decisions on top of it. The next conversation you have with them should be about this document — what they agree with, what they want to push back on, what technical questions it raises.

If they push back ("can't I just talk it through with them?"), hold the line:

> You can also talk it through. But send the doc first, in writing. Conversation is lossy; a document is not. Your co-founder will spend half a Saturday with the doc and come back with sharper questions than they'd ask over coffee.

## End of session

After the resources and the handoff restatement, stop. Don't summarize. Don't ask if there's anything else. The closing has shape — let it land.

If the founder has follow-up questions, answer them. If they want to revise either document, revise it and re-run the affected Phase 6 passes on the slice that changed.

They have two documents, an assignment, a handoff to make this week, and the next thing to read. That's the point.
