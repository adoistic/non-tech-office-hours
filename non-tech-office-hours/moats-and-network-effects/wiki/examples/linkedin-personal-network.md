# LinkedIn — Personal Network Effects (Identity Graph)

**Concept:** [[personal-network-effects]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

LinkedIn is a professional identity graph launched in 2003 where users maintain a public, real-name profile representing their career history, skills, and connections. Employers, recruiters, and professionals use it to signal and verify professional identity. Microsoft acquired it in 2016 for $26B.

## The mechanism

- A user's professional reputation and work history are stored in a portable-looking but practically sticky profile tied to their real name and employment records.
- Each connection adds visibility into second- and third-degree networks, making the graph more valuable the more complete the coverage of a given industry is.
- Recruiters pay for access to the talent graph; talent joins to be visible to recruiters — a reinforcing two-sided pull layered on top of the personal network effect.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders who say "we're building the LinkedIn for X profession" typically overestimate portability and underestimate what LinkedIn actually is. LinkedIn's moat is not the social feed or the messaging feature; it is that the professional world has agreed that LinkedIn is the canonical record of professional identity. A new entrant must convince an entire professional community to maintain a parallel identity record, which is costly even when LinkedIn's UX is frustrating. The analog also usually misses that LinkedIn's recruiter-side revenue is its actual business model; without a paying demand side willing to spend on talent search, a professional identity graph is a social product with no revenue path.
