# Craigslist — Two-Sided Marketplace

**Concept:** [[two-sided-marketplace]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

Craigslist is a classified ad platform launched in 1995 that offers free listings for most categories. Buyers and sellers of local goods, rentals, jobs, and services transact through it. It operates in hundreds of cities worldwide with minimal product investment — the interface has barely changed in 25 years.

## The mechanism

- Free-side listing eliminates friction for supply: sellers post at zero cost, so any buyer-demand growth immediately induces supply growth.
- Local density drives the network effect: a listing in a specific city is only valuable to buyers in that city, making Craigslist dominant city by city rather than globally.
- Switching requires both parties to switch simultaneously across a fragmented local market; the coordination cost protects Craigslist even against visibly better products.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders who say "we're disrupting Craigslist" have been saying this since 2007, and Craigslist is still dominant in most local categories. The analog fails when founders assume the moat is product quality — it is not. Craigslist persists because its local liquidity in each city is deep enough that a new entrant cannot offer comparable fill rates without already having both sides present. The free-to-list model also matters: any competitor who charges for listings is automatically at a disadvantage for supply acquisition. Founders should also note that Craigslist has deliberately kept the product bad to avoid adding features that would attract regulatory attention or competitive signal-reading.
