# Google — Language Network Effects

**Concept:** [[language-network-effects]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

Google Search launched in 1998 as a page-ranking search engine and became the dominant global web search product. Within a few years, "to Google" became a verb in everyday speech in dozens of languages, embedding Google into the act of searching itself rather than leaving it as a brand name for one of several search options.

## The mechanism

- When a word becomes the generic verb for an action ("Google it"), social cues actively direct users to that product even when alternatives exist; pulling out a phone to use Bing when someone asks you to "Google it" is socially marked behavior.
- Language network effects compound as adoption widens: the more people use "Google" as a verb, the more the verb reinforces the product as the default, which drives more adoption.
- Language is sticky in ways that product features are not: even if a competitor builds a technically superior search engine, the verb does not transfer.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders who say "we want to become the verb for X" typically confuse cause and effect. Google did not set out to become a verb; it achieved verb status by being so dominant for so long that language naturally encoded it. Verb status is an outcome of winning, not a strategy for winning. The other miss: language network effects are considered among the weaker and less durable of the network effect types. Google's current dominance rests on data network effects and distribution, not on the fact that people say "google it." A startup relying on language network effects as its primary moat is relying on something that lags adoption rather than drives it.
