# AT&T — Physical Network Effects

**Concept:** [[physical-network-effects]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

AT&T built the dominant US telephone network in the early 20th century by laying copper wire to connect every home, business, and exchange in a given locale. Customers paid for access to everyone else on the network, not just for the phone device itself. By 1908, Theodore Vail had articulated why no competitor could catch them in any city where AT&T held density.

## The mechanism

- Every new subscriber added calling reach for all existing subscribers, compounding value geometrically as density grew.
- Physical infrastructure (wire in the ground, exchanges, switching equipment) required massive upfront capital that no rival could replicate city by city once AT&T held the local monopoly.
- Embedding locked users in: switching required the other party to also switch, making unilateral exit irrational.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders citing AT&T as a comparable almost always miss that the physical moat required literal infrastructure investment that made replication prohibitively expensive. If a founder's product does not require physical nodes tied to geography, the analog breaks immediately. AT&T's durability also depended on regulatory capture: once it became a regulated monopoly, it was protected from competition by law, not just by network density. "We're like AT&T for X" only holds if the founder can explain what the physical infrastructure equivalent is, who bears the capital cost of deploying it, and why a competitor cannot bypass it with a different physical or wireless approach.
