# Scale Economies

**Category:** Helmer Power
**Source:** raw/yc-helmer-7-powers-transcript.md

## Definition

Scale economies exist when a large fixed-cost investment — infrastructure, crawl, training compute — is spread across so many units that the per-unit cost drops below what any smaller competitor can match. As Jared Friedman frames it: "You've invested a lot of money to build something really big, and as a result you can offer the service cheaper than anybody else."

## Mechanism

- Fixed costs are amortized across a large customer base, so the unit economics of the incumbent improve as volume grows while the entrant's stay flat.
- The capital investment acts as a barrier to entry: a new competitor must absorb the full fixed cost before serving a single customer.
- Lower unit cost enables lower pricing or higher margins, both of which compound the scale advantage over time.

## When it applies

- The business requires large, non-divisible infrastructure investment (training compute, physical delivery networks, web crawls).
- The same fixed asset can serve many customers with little marginal cost per additional customer.
- Capital requirements for the infrastructure are high enough to deter well-funded but not mega-funded entrants.

## Kill-test

Could a new, well-funded competitor build equivalent infrastructure by spending three times what you spent, or does the investment advantage compound non-linearly with cumulative spend? If a 3x check buys parity, your scale moat is thin.

## AI-native erosion check

At the application layer, scale economies are mostly absent in the AI era — software replication is cheap and LLM APIs flatten marginal cost. The power lives at the model layer: training a frontier LLM is capital-intensive and only a handful of organizations can afford it. DeepSeek's 2025 announcement rattled this assumption, suggesting training cost may fall faster than expected, which would compress the scale advantage even for the labs. For application-layer startups, scale economies are rarely the primary moat; process power and switching costs carry more weight.
