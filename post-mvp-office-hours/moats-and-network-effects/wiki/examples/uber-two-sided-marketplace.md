# Uber — Two-Sided Marketplace

**Concept:** [[two-sided-marketplace]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

Mobile-app-mediated car booking, drivers paid per trip, riders paying app-set fares. Launched 2010 in San Francisco, scaled by city. Uber connected riders who needed on-demand transportation with drivers willing to work flexible hours using personal vehicles.

## The mechanism

- Uber's network effect was local: drivers and riders had to be in the same city at the same time for the network to have any value.
- Each city required achieving a density threshold before wait times dropped below the level where riders preferred it to taxis; below that threshold, the product was worse than incumbents.
- Supply (drivers) and demand (riders) are asymmetric: drivers maximize income by working peak hours, which is also when riders need supply most, creating natural alignment that stabilizes the liquidity problem.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders citing Uber as a comparable usually fail to acknowledge that Uber's network effect was local (drivers and riders in the same city) and liquidity-dependent (the network only worked once each city hit a density threshold). If the founder's "Uber for X" doesn't have a local liquidity mechanism, the analog breaks. Also: Uber's actual moat in 2026 is regulatory position, brand recognition, and cornered driver supply in major markets, not network effects per se — the network effect was the on-ramp, not the durable moat.
