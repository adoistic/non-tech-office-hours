# Excel — Expertise Network Effects

**Concept:** [[expertise-network-effects]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

Microsoft Excel is a spreadsheet application launched in 1985 that became the dominant tool for financial modeling, data analysis, and business operations across virtually every industry. Power users invest years learning formulas, pivot tables, VBA macros, and modeling conventions that are specific to Excel's implementation.

## The mechanism

- Employers require Excel proficiency on job postings because their existing teams and processes are built around it; this creates demand-side pressure that forces new workers into the ecosystem.
- Power-user expertise (advanced financial models, VBA automation, complex formula architectures) is largely non-transferable to competitors, creating individual lock-in on top of organizational lock-in.
- The sheer volume of shared Excel files across organizations creates a file-format dependency: a team cannot switch to a competing tool without also converting or abandoning years of spreadsheet infrastructure.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders building "a better spreadsheet" or "the Excel for X" consistently underestimate how much of Excel's moat is in the accumulated human expertise, not the product. Google Sheets is technically comparable and free, yet Excel retains enterprise dominance because organizations have millions of person-hours of invested skill and file infrastructure that cannot be migrated. The insight for founders: expertise network effects require that the tool be hard enough to master that expertise creates real career value, and common enough that employers mandate it. A niche tool used by 500 people has no expertise network effect — the labor market loop never forms.
