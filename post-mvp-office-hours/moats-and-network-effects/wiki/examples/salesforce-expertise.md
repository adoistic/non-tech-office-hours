# Salesforce — Expertise Network Effects

**Concept:** [[expertise-network-effects]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

Salesforce is a CRM platform launched in 1999 that manages sales pipelines, customer records, and business processes for enterprise and mid-market companies. It added Trailhead (a free online learning platform for Salesforce skills) in 2014 and built an ecosystem of certified administrators, developers, and consultants who earn their livelihood from Salesforce expertise.

## The mechanism

- Every Salesforce-certified admin or developer who enters the labor market makes Salesforce more valuable to employers: they can hire, replace, and train staff more easily when the skill set is standardized.
- Trailhead produced millions of credentialed users who had direct financial incentive (higher salaries, consulting fees) to deepen and maintain their Salesforce expertise, making the ecosystem self-reinforcing.
- Companies standardize on Salesforce partly because other companies standardize on it: interoperability, shared consultants, and industry-standard integrations compound over time.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders who say "we're building expertise network effects like Salesforce" typically skip the most load-bearing part: Salesforce spent years deliberately building a certification ecosystem and a labor market that priced the credential. Expertise network effects require employers to demand the skill on job postings and candidates to supply it on resumes — that labor-market loop does not form organically on a short timeline. A new enterprise software product with a learning portal is not Salesforce; it becomes Salesforce only after enough companies mandate it and enough consultants build careers around it.
