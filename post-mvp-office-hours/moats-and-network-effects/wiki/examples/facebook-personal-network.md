# Facebook — Personal Network Effects

**Concept:** [[personal-network-effects]]
**Source:** raw/nfx-network-effects-manual.md

## What they built

Facebook built a social graph tied to real-world identity, initially for US college students, expanding globally after 2006. Users create profiles linked to their actual name, then connect with people they know in person. The product's value is entirely a function of who else is on it.

## The mechanism

- A user's social graph is stored on Facebook's servers and is not portable: leaving means abandoning accumulated connections, photos, and history.
- Each new user makes the network more valuable for every existing user who knows them, creating compounding density in each real-world social cluster.
- Real-name identity raises the cost of maintaining a parallel identity on a competing platform, reducing multi-tenanting pressure compared to pseudonymous networks.

## Why the analog often fails

Founders who say "we're building the Facebook for X community" almost always underestimate how complete Facebook's capture of the real-world social graph already is. The analog fails because Facebook's moat is not just that people are there; it is that people's accumulated relational history is there and costs nothing to store, making the switching cost nearly permanent. A new entrant must convince users to reconstruct their social graph from scratch, which almost never happens without a catastrophic loss of trust in the incumbent. If the founder's "X community" already uses Facebook Groups or Instagram, the analog does not just fail — it competes directly against the incumbent's own market extension.
