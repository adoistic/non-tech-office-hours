# Counter-Positioning

**Category:** Helmer Power
**Source:** raw/yc-helmer-7-powers-transcript.md

## Definition

Counter-Positioning is doing something that is difficult for the incumbent you compete with to copy because copying would cannibalize their existing business. As Garry Tan defines it: "The definition of [counter-positioning] is doing something that is difficult for the incumbent that you are competing with to copy because it would cannibalize their business." The structural inability to respond (not merely unwillingness) is what makes it a durable power.

## Mechanism

- The incumbent's business model creates a trap: adopting the new model would reduce revenue from the existing model before the new model produces equivalent revenue.
- Per-seat SaaS pricing is the clearest current example: AI agents that automate work reduce headcount, which reduces seats, which reduces the incumbent's revenue — the more successful the agent, the more the incumbent's revenue falls.
- Non-founder-controlled incumbents face compounded inertia: boards and public market expectations make self-cannibalization politically nearly impossible.

## When it applies

- You have a fundamentally different business model, not just a better product.
- The incumbent's adoption of your model would require them to shrink a revenue line that currently funds their operations.
- The structural conflict is visible in the incumbent's incentive structure, not just their culture.

## Kill-test

Does your business model create an outcome a competitor literally cannot replicate without harming their existing business? Name the specific harm.

## AI-native erosion check

Counter-Positioning held up well in the AI era because it is rooted in business model structure, not technical capability. AI does not change whether a per-seat pricing incumbent can afford to adopt per-outcome pricing — that decision is constrained by investor expectations and org incentives, not by what is technically possible. The Zendesk/Intercom vs. AI-native customer support story is the live version: seat-based incumbents know per-outcome pricing is the right model; they cannot get there because it would destroy near-term revenue.
