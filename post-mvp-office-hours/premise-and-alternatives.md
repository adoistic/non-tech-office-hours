# Premise Challenge & Alternatives

Run both of these before writing the artifact drafts. Neither is skippable.

For Searching/Approaching routes, the alternatives focus on customer-discovery and wedge clarity. For Claims-PMF/Fundraise-ready routes, the alternatives include capital-allocation choices (raise/don't raise).

---

## Phase 3 — Premise Challenge

Before proposing solutions, surface the premises the founder is operating on. They are usually invisible to the person holding them. Your job is to drag them into daylight and force the founder to either agree explicitly or rethink.

Ask yourself, then surface to the founder:

1. **Is this the right problem?** Could a different framing yield a dramatically simpler or more useful solution?
2. **What does the data actually say?** Don't let the founder narrate the data — ask to see it. Cohort curves, conversion rates, expansion revenue, churn. What does the data say versus what the founder says?
3. **What existing tools or workarounds are customers still using?** Even with the MVP live, customers may be routing around it. What are they doing instead, and why?
4. **Who pays, and is the payment pattern holding?** In pre-MVP, this is aspirational. Post-MVP, you have evidence. What does the payment data actually show?
5. **What does the founder believe about the customer that the data has not confirmed?** The most dangerous premises are the ones the founder formed before the MVP and has not re-examined since.
6. **What about distribution?** Is the current acquisition channel repeatable and scalable, or is the founder still relying on founder network? What happens when that well runs dry?
7. **What's the compounding advantage?** If the MVP has been live long enough to accumulate data, is that data becoming an advantage — or is it sitting idle? What learning loop is operating?

### Stricter rule: 5-7 premises, not 3-5

Each premise must be a statement that, **if wrong, would change the design.** "Users want a fast app" is not a premise — it's a truism. "The cohort that activated on Feature X has 40% better 90-day retention, meaning X is the wedge, not the dashboard" is a premise. "Customers in segment A churn at 3x the rate of segment B, meaning segment A is a distraction we should stop selling into" is a premise.

Output them as a numbered list, each phrased as a clear declarative the founder can either agree with or push back on:

```
PREMISES:
1. [declarative statement] — do you agree?
2. [declarative statement] — do you agree?
3. [declarative statement] — do you agree?
4. [declarative statement] — do you agree?
5. [declarative statement] — do you agree?
```

### The agree-back gate

For each premise, ask the founder to **rephrase it in their own words** before they agree. If they cannot, the premise is not yet shared understanding — it's just words on a screen. Push again.

If they disagree with a premise, **revise your understanding** and loop back. Don't paper over disagreement. A premise the founder doesn't actually believe is a landmine waiting to detonate when the data contradicts it six weeks from now.

### Common premises worth surfacing — post-MVP edition

These apply once the product is live and users exist. Surface the ones that apply. Don't force premises that don't fit.

- **The PMF premise.** "We have found product-market fit in the current wedge — the retention curve confirms it." (If true, the strategic question is how to widen. If false, the strategic question is what to change.)
- **The compounding-learning-loop premise.** "Our AI-native cost structure and closed-loop data means our iteration speed is compounding — each cohort improves conversion and retention faster than the last. Competitors without this loop fall behind."
- **The segment-clarity premise.** "We now know which customer segment has the clearest signal and we are intentionally concentrating there, not averaging across segments."
- **The distribution-repeatability premise.** "Our current acquisition channel is repeatable and does not depend on the founder's personal network. If it does depend on founder network, that is a constraint, not a distribution strategy."
- **The runway-as-optionality premise.** "Our AI-native cost structure means we can extend runway by N months without raising, and that optionality is worth more than dilution at the current valuation."
- **The incumbent-response premise.** "We have been live long enough that incumbents have had a chance to respond. Their response (or non-response) tells us something real about our window."
- **The wedge-expansion premise.** "The next wedge to attack is [X], and the data from the current wedge supports moving there rather than somewhere else."

## Phase 4 — Alternatives Generation (MANDATORY, PICK 2-3 FROM THE MENU)

Post-MVP, the alternative set is not about what to build — it is about what strategic move to make next. Present the following 6-option menu to the founder and **require them to pick 2-3 options** that match their current situation. Do not pick for them. Do not present 3 pre-selected options and ask which they prefer.

### The 6-option menu

**1. Pivot the wedge** — keep the team, change the wedge. You have evidence the current wedge is not working (retention is flat-to-zero, customers churn after trial, the use case is too narrow). Move to an adjacent problem with the same customer or the same problem with a different customer. This is not a full pivot — you are keeping what you have learned and changing the application of it.

**2. Double down on what's working** — kill everything else, ride the strongest signal. You have a cohort or a segment where retention is real and compounding. Stop building features for the segments that don't show that signal. Concentrate resources on the one that does. This is the most common correct answer for a Claims-PMF-stage founder who is still hedging.

**3. Kill a feature or segment** — reduce surface area. You are serving too many customers or too many use cases, and the cognitive and engineering load is preventing you from doing any of them well. Name the feature or segment that, if removed, would free up the most capacity with the least revenue impact. The post-MVP version of this is often "stop selling to [segment X]."

**4. Change distribution** — same product, different channel. Your product may be working but your acquisition channel is founder-dependent, low-repeatability, or about to plateau. What would happen if you sold through a different channel — partner, reseller, marketplace, community, bottom-up PLG instead of top-down sales? Distribution is often the actual bottleneck, not the product.

**5. Raise now** — capital is the unblocker. You have the signal (retention, revenue, cohort curves) and there is a specific milestone that capital unlocks that you cannot reach organically in time. Name the milestone. Name why timing matters. Name what raising now costs in dilution versus waiting. If you cannot name a concrete milestone, you are not ready to raise — you are just nervous about the runway.

**6. Don't raise yet** — runway is fine, raising would distract. You have enough runway to reach a materially better valuation marker. Raising now would cost dilution you can avoid. The founder's time spent fundraising is time not spent with customers. Quantify the runway extension and name the milestone you would hit before raising that would change your terms.

### How to use the menu

Present all 6 options. Then stop.

Ask the founder to pick **2 or 3** of these alternatives that fit their current situation. Tell them to also name **which premises from Phase 3 each chosen alternative is betting on** — an alternative without a premise bet is a preference, not a strategy.

Format the request like this:

```
From the 6 alternatives above, pick 2 or 3 that match where you are.
For each one you pick, name:
  - Which premises from our Phase 3 list it is betting on
  - What must be true for this alternative to be the right call
  - What would tell you in 30-60 days that you made the wrong call
```

If a chosen alternative claims a moat, walk `moats-and-network-effects/wiki/concepts/` to name the specific Helmer power or NFX network-effect type, and confirm the mechanism is real, not aspirational.

### Avoid these failure modes

- **Three flavors of the same thing.** If the founder picks "pivot the wedge," "kill a segment," and "change distribution" and they all collapse into "do less of what we're doing" — that is one move with cosmetic variation. Push for genuine strategic differentiation across the picks.
- **A strawman option.** Don't steer the founder toward a choice by making the other options obviously bad.
- **Hedging the recommendation.** After the founder picks 2-3 and articulates the premise bets, you must offer a recommendation: which of their chosen alternatives is the right first move, and why. Use the data from the diagnostic as the reason. If you can't recommend one, the alternatives aren't differentiated enough.
- **Sycophantic re-routing** — re-selecting alternatives without new evidence (also covered in principles.md). If the founder pushes back on an alternative assessment, require a new signal before changing your recommendation. "I just feel like Option 2 is more exciting" is not a signal.
- **Ignoring the data** — choosing an alternative that contradicts the cohort retention curve, customer interviews, or unit economics. If data exists, it constrains the alternative set. An alternative that the retention data makes implausible is not a real alternative — name the contradiction and force the founder to resolve it before proceeding.

### STOP HERE

After presenting the 6-option menu, **wait for the founder to pick 2-3 alternatives and articulate the premise bets behind each.** Do not move on until they have done both. Writing the artifact draft without this explicit commitment is the failure mode this gate prevents.

A typical response names 2 options and a rough rationale. Push for the third if they only name two. Push for the premise bets if they skip that step. A merged version (e.g., "Option 2 but executed through Option 4's channel") is valid — confirm it briefly, then proceed.

## After alternatives are approved

Move to **Phase 4 — Strategy** ([strategy.md](strategy.md)). The alternative the founder picked is *how* they will execute. The strategy phase is the bet about *why* it wins on a chosen playing field — and the logic that must hold for the bet to land. Without the strategy, the chosen approach is a plan, not a strategy. Don't let the founder skip past it.
